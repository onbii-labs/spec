# Vision

## Why Onbii Exists

Onbii began with a simple frustration: **important knowledge is easy to lose, even when it was technically captured.**

A good conversation happens. A useful meeting ends. A phone call contains a promise. A podcast mentions exactly the thing you have been thinking about. A voice memo captures an idea before it disappears.

Later, you want to know what was said, who was involved, what it connects to, and whether it matters now.

Too often, that knowledge is either gone, buried in an app, trapped behind an export flow, reduced to a flat summary, or disconnected from everything else you know.

> Onbii exists so that knowledge people choose to keep can remain accessible, connected, and useful over time.

## Frictionless Access

The first promise is simple:

> When something matters, it should be easy to get it into your knowledge system and easy to find again later.

That does not mean every capture must be automatic. It does mean the system should reduce the constant friction of deciding where things go, how they are named, how they are filed, how they are processed, and how they can be found again.

The feeling should be: **it is in there, I can deal with it later, and I will not lose it.**

## Connections Over Notes

The goal is **not** to create prettier meeting notes.

The goal is to help **knowledge build on itself.**

A useful system should help answer questions like:

- What did I discuss with John?
- Who else has mentioned this?
- Did Peter or Paul talk about something related?
- Which conversations led to this decision?
- What did I promise to follow up on?
- Has something changed since we last discussed this?
- Which people, projects, documents, and ideas are connected?

This is closer to a memory-aware CRM than to a note-taking app, but it should not be limited to business relationships. It should work for conversations, ideas, research, projects, documents, media, and personal knowledge.

> This is one of the places where AI can be genuinely useful: not as a novelty, but as a way to reduce mental load.

It can help surface what matters, connect related threads, remember follow-ups, and clear space in a person's head without forcing them to manually maintain every relationship.

## Knowledge That Accumulates Meaning

Most capture tools treat a recording as finished once it has a transcript and summary.

Onbii should treat captured knowledge as something that can **gain meaning over time.**

A conversation may become more important after a later meeting. A podcast may become relevant once a project changes direction. A person may connect two topics that were previously separate. A market development may matter because of an assumption captured months earlier.

The knowledge itself should be able to keep accumulating **context, links, corrections, topics, classifications, and interpretations.**

## Owned By The Person

Personal knowledge should not depend on staying inside **one company's product.**

People should be able to choose their recorder, their storage, their sync, their AI, and their applications without losing the knowledge they have already built.

If a user changes tools, the knowledge should come with them. If a better model appears, the knowledge should be usable with it. If an application disappears, the archive should remain understandable.

## Human Understandable

The system should not require ordinary users to understand graph databases, vector stores, backend services, or file choreography.

The technical architecture may need source adapters, bundles, provenance, object identity, indexes, and processing pipelines. Those are necessary implementation details. They should make the experience simpler, not become the experience.

The user-facing promise is that knowledge remains **readable, searchable, connected, and under the user's control.**

## Many Tools, One Memory

Onbii is not meant to replace every tool people already use.

A recorder may be good at recording. A note app may be good at browsing. A mobile app may be good at capture. An AI assistant may be good at recall. A search tool may be good at retrieval. A graph view may be good at showing relationships.

The value of Onbii is that these tools can contribute to and work from the **same underlying knowledge** instead of creating separate silos.

## What Success Looks Like

Success is **not** that everyone uses the same app.

Success is when a person can rely on their own knowledge system the way they rely on contacts, calendar, or email: something they can return to, search, connect, and build on.

Success is being able to say:

> "I spoke to John about this. Peter and Paul were interested too. There was a related podcast. This connects to that project. Here is what changed since then."

That is the vision: **frictionless access to knowledge that keeps becoming more connected, more useful, and still belongs to the person who created or collected it.**
